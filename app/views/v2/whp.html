Use natural flood management to reduce your risk of flooding

Find out how to work with natural processes to capture water or slow its flow and who to contact to support you.

From:

Published:

Natural flood management reduces flood risk by protecting, restoring and imitating natural processes.

It captures and stores water which can:

  • delay when flooding begins giving more time to prepare
  • reduce the impact of flooding

It can be a low maintenance, long-term way for communities to reduce flood risk in an area while increasing biodiversity and improving the environment.

Making changes upstream can slow and capture water before it reaches areas downstream. It is most effective when used across the local drainage area of a river known as a catchment. On the coast it can be used to reduce the risk of flooding from waves and storms.

It can be used on its own or with formal flood defence engineering for example walls and banks.

Types of natural flood management

Natural flood management restores or follows natural processes, you can:

  • plant trees and manage woodland
  • improve soil
  • create areas to temporarily store water
  • restore connections between rivers and floodplains
  • restore salt marshes and mudflats
  • build sand dunes

The types of natural flood management you will use will depend on your local area and the amount of time they take to reduce the risk of flooding will vary.

Make small changes at home

You can contribute to capturing water and slowing its flow by:

  • installing water butts
  • reducing hard paving around your property
  • installing green roofs
  • building ponds and rain gardens

Find out more about how to use these sustainable drainage systems.

Work together

By working together with your local community, the Environment Agency and non-governmental organisations you can get involved in ongoing projects or start your own.

You may need a permission to undertake some types of natural flood management.

Get support

Email the natural flood management team or contact the Environment Agency to get advice about natural flood management in your area.
